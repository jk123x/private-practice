## Business Structure

**Full guide:** privatepracticeguide.com.au/guides/business-structure

**Sole trader vs Pty Ltd: the short version**

Most new practitioners start as a sole trader. It's simpler, cheaper to set up, and appropriate when you're testing whether private practice works for you.

| Factor | Sole Trader | Pty Ltd |
|--------|------------|---------|
| Setup cost | ~$0 (free ABN) | $500-$1,500 (ASIC registration + legal) |
| Annual cost | Minimal | $300+ ASIC fee + higher accounting fees |
| Tax rate | Personal marginal rate (up to 45%) | 25% company tax |
| Asset protection | None (personal assets at risk) | Limited liability |
| Complexity | Low | Higher (separate tax return, compliance) |
| Good for | Year 1, income under ~$120K net | Higher income, asset protection needed |

**When to switch:** Talk to your accountant when your net income consistently exceeds $120,000-$150,000. The tax savings from a company structure typically outweigh the additional compliance costs at that point.

**Key steps:** Register for an ABN (free, immediate), register a business name if different from your personal name ($39 for 1 year, $92 for 3 years via ASIC), and open a business bank account.

## Medicare CDM Billing

**Full guide:** privatepracticeguide.com.au/guides/cdm-billing

**How CDM billing works under GPCCMP (from July 2025)**

The old GPMP/TCA system was replaced by GPCCMP (GP Chronic Condition Management Plan) in July 2025. Most online resources still reference the old system.

**The process:**
1. A GP identifies a patient with a chronic condition who would benefit from allied health services
2. The GP creates a GPCCMP for the patient
3. The GP refers the patient to specific allied health providers
4. The patient receives up to 5 Medicare-subsidised allied health sessions per calendar year
5. The 5-session cap is shared across ALL allied health providers (not 5 per provider)

**Key points:**
- The patient needs a valid GPCCMP and referral to see you under Medicare CDM
- Each session must be at least 20 minutes face-to-face
- You bill using your profession's specific Medicare item number
- Medicare rebate for individual CDM session: approximately $56.70
- Telehealth CDM sessions attract the same rebate
- Group sessions (60+ minutes, 2-12 patients) have a lower rebate (~$18.90)
- You need a Medicare provider number for each location where you practice

**Common billing mistakes:**
- Billing without a valid referral
- Exceeding the 5-session cap without checking
- Using the wrong item number for your profession
- Not meeting the 20-minute minimum face-to-face requirement

## Provider Numbers

**Full guide:** privatepracticeguide.com.au/guides/provider-number

**Getting your Medicare provider number**

You need a Medicare provider number to bill Medicare for CDM services. The number is location-specific, meaning you need a separate provider number for each practice location.

**Steps:**
1. Set up a PRODA account (online identity verification with Services Australia)
2. Complete the HW093 application form through HPOS (Health Professional Online Services)
3. Submit with required documentation (proof of registration, practice address, qualifications)
4. Wait for processing (typically 2-6 weeks, can be longer)

**Important details:**
- Apply at least 4-6 weeks before you plan to start seeing CDM patients
- You need proof of your practice address (room rental agreement, lease, or letter from the practice owner)
- If you work across multiple locations, you need a provider number for each
- Home visit and telehealth services use your primary practice location's provider number
- If your application is rejected, it's usually a documentation issue. Fix the specific problem and resubmit

**Location-specific trap:** Your provider number is tied to a physical address. If you move practice locations, you need to update your provider number. This is often forgotten and can cause billing issues.

## Practice Costs

**Full guide:** privatepracticeguide.com.au/guides/practice-costs

**What it actually costs to start**

Total startup costs for a solo allied health practice range from approximately $5,000 to $30,000, depending on your profession, location, and setup choice.

**Mandatory costs (everyone pays these):**

| Item | Approximate Cost |
|------|-----------------|
| AHPRA registration (or equivalent) | $300-$800/year |
| Professional indemnity insurance | $500-$1,500/year |
| Public liability insurance | $300-$600/year |
| Professional association membership | $300-$700/year |
| ABN registration | Free |
| Business name registration | $39-$92 |
| **Total mandatory** | **~$1,500-$3,700** |

**Practice setup costs (varies by arrangement):**

| Item | Room Rental | Home Practice | Standalone Lease |
|------|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Premises cost | $50-$150/day | Minimal | $500-$2,000+/week |
| Fitout | Minimal | $500-$2,000 | $5,000-$30,000+ |
| Equipment | $500-$5,000 | $500-$5,000 | $5,000-$20,000+ |
| Signage | N/A | N/A | $500-$2,000 |

**Ongoing monthly costs:**

| Item | Approximate Range |
|------|------------------|
| Room rental | $800-$2,400 |
| Practice management software | $0-$55 |
| Accounting software | $25-$65 |
| Internet/phone | $80-$150 |
| Consumables | $50-$300 |
| Cleaning | $100-$300 |
| Accountant/bookkeeper | $150-$400 |
| **Total ongoing** | **~$1,200-$3,700/month** |

**The bottom line:** A room-rental setup in an existing clinic is the lowest-risk way to start. You avoid fitout costs, share some infrastructure, and can scale up or change location without major financial commitment. Most new solo practitioners start this way.
