# Chapter 2: Setting Up Your Practice

> [!tldr]
> Before you see your first patient, you need a place to work, software to manage it, insurance to protect you, and your Medicare claiming configured. This chapter walks through each decision: where to practise, which software to use, what insurance to get, and how to set up telehealth. Most of it can be sorted in a week if you know what you're choosing between.

> [!key-number]
> **$200–$600/week** typical room rental in an existing clinic
> **$0–$55/month** practice management software range
> **$1,000–$2,500/yr** professional indemnity insurance

Chapter 1 covered whether going private makes financial sense. This chapter covers the practical decisions between "I've decided" and "I'm ready to see patients." There's a sequence that matters here, because some steps depend on others being done first.

The rough order: location first (you need an address for your provider number), then insurance (you need it before seeing anyone), then software (you need it configured before your first appointment), then Medicare claiming setup (you need it before your first CDM patient). Telehealth can be added any time.

## Where to Practise

This is your biggest ongoing cost and the decision that's hardest to undo. Three main options, and for most new solo practitioners, only one of them makes sense to start with.

### Room rental in an existing clinic

You rent a treatment room in someone else's clinic, usually by the day or half-day. The clinic provides the room, reception area, and typically a waiting room. You bring your own equipment, your own patients, and your own brand.

**What it costs:** $200–$600 per week for 2-3 days, depending on the city and suburb. Metro Sydney and Melbourne are at the higher end. Regional areas and smaller cities are lower. Some clinics charge a flat daily rate ($80–$150/day). Others take a percentage of your billings (typically 25-35%).

**Why it works for starting out.** Your fixed costs stay low. You're not locked into a lease. If the location doesn't work, you can move without breaking a contract. You get the benefits of an established clinic (foot traffic, reception, shared facilities) without the overhead of running one. And the room is already set up for clinical work, so your fitout costs are close to zero.

**What to watch for.** Read the rental agreement carefully. Check: who books the room when you're not there? Can they put another practitioner in your profession in the clinic? What's the notice period if either of you wants to end the arrangement? Is the room available on the specific days you want? Can you put your name on signage?

> [!tip]
> Ask about exclusivity. Some room rental agreements let the clinic owner install another physio (or whatever your profession is) in the same space on your days off. That practitioner is now competing with you for the same local GPs. If you can negotiate profession exclusivity for the clinic, it's worth a slightly higher rate.

### Leasing your own space

You sign a commercial lease and build out your own clinic. Full control over the space, the brand, the hours, everything.

**What it costs:** $25,000–$80,000+ in fitout costs (treatment rooms, waiting area, reception, signage, accessibility compliance), plus $1,500–$4,000/month in rent, plus utilities, cleaning, and maintenance. Commercial leases are typically 3-5 years with personal guarantees.

**When it makes sense:** When you're running at capacity and want to expand. When you're planning to hire other practitioners. When you've been renting rooms for 18+ months and the economics of your own space work out. Not when you're starting out.

> [!warning]
> A commercial lease is a personal financial commitment, even through a Pty Ltd. Landlords almost always require a director's personal guarantee. If the practice fails, you're still paying rent for the remaining lease term. This is why room rental exists: it lets you build your patient base before taking on that kind of obligation.

### Home office

Working from a dedicated room in your home. Legal in most states for some allied health professions, but with significant restrictions.

**What it costs:** Minimal, which is the appeal. You can claim a proportion of your home expenses as a business deduction.

**The restrictions:** You'll need council approval in most local government areas (check your specific LGA). The room must meet health and safety standards for clinical use. Patients need accessible entry. You need appropriate signage (which your council may or may not allow in a residential area). Parking becomes your problem. And some professional associations or insurers have specific requirements about home-based practice.

**Who it works for:** Practitioners doing primarily telehealth or home visits who need an admin base. Practitioners in regional areas where room rental options are limited. It's harder to make work for professions that need treatment tables, equipment, or clinical-grade facilities.

For most people starting out: **rent a room.** It's the lowest-risk option and it gives you a real clinical environment from day one.

## Choosing a Location

Whether you're renting a room or eventually leasing, location affects how quickly you build a patient base. A few things to check before you commit.

**Demographics.** Your CDM patients are predominantly older adults with chronic conditions. Areas with an older population generate more GPCCMPs and more allied health referrals. Check ABS census data or .id community profiles for your target area. You're looking at median age, chronic disease prevalence, and the proportion of residents over 55.

**Competition.** How many practices in your profession are already within 5-10km? A handful is healthy. Fifteen is a warning. Use Google Maps, HotDoc, and your professional association directory to map what's already there. If the area is saturated, either find a gap in what's offered (a specialty niche, telehealth, home visits) or look at neighbouring suburbs.

**Proximity to GPs.** Your CDM referrals come from GP clinics. How many are nearby? GPs refer to practitioners close to their patients. A location surrounded by GP clinics is a location surrounded by potential referral sources.

**Accessibility and parking.** Your patients include older adults and people with chronic conditions. Ground floor or lift access matters. Dedicated parking matters. If patients have to climb stairs or circle the block for 10 minutes, they'll go somewhere easier.

> [!tip]
> Before signing anything, drive to the location during business hours. Is there parking? Can someone with a walking frame get from the car park to the front door without steps? Is there signage visible from the street? These things affect your patient flow more than the room itself.

## Practice Management Software

You need software that handles bookings, patient records, invoicing, and Medicare claiming. All four. A notebook and Excel spreadsheet will last about a week before things start falling through.

There are several options in the Australian allied health market. None of them is perfect, but all of them are better than doing it manually.

### What to look for

- **Medicare claiming integration.** Can you submit claims directly from the software? This is non-negotiable. Manual claiming through HPOS is slow and error-prone at volume.
- **NDIS invoicing.** Built-in NDIS support item numbers and invoice templates. Relevant for all CDM professions, essential for EP, OT, and speech path.
- **Online booking.** Patients expect to book online. If the software doesn't support it natively, you'll need a separate booking tool.
- **Clinical notes.** Templates, progress notes, treatment plans. Your clinical documentation lives here.
- **Reporting.** Revenue tracking, appointment statistics, cancellation rates. You need this data to know how your practice is performing.

### The options

| Software | Monthly cost | Best for | Medicare claiming | NDIS | Online booking |
|----------|-------------|----------|-------------------|------|----------------|
| Halaxy | Free (core), paid add-ons | Solo practitioners starting out | Yes (direct) | Yes | Yes |
| Cliniko | $45/user | Physio, established practices | Yes (via Tyro/Medicare Easyclaim) | Basic | Yes (via integration) |
| Nookal | $55/month | Physio, multi-practitioner | Yes (direct) | Yes | Yes |
| CorePlus | $5–$45/user | Budget-conscious, NDIS-heavy | Yes (direct) | Strong | Yes |

**Halaxy** is where most solo practitioners start. The core product is free, which matters when you're watching every dollar. Medicare claiming is built in. NDIS invoicing works. Online booking works. The free tier has limitations (fewer customisations, Halaxy branding on patient communications), but for a solo practice in Year 1, it does the job. You can upgrade to paid plans later or switch software entirely once you know what you need.

**Cliniko** is popular with physios and well-established. The interface is clean and the clinical notes templates are strong. At $45/month it's not expensive, but it's not free either, and Medicare claiming requires a Tyro terminal or Easyclaim setup rather than being built directly into the software. If you're a physio and you've used Cliniko in a previous role, the familiarity might be worth the cost.

**Nookal** is built for allied health and handles multi-location, multi-practitioner setups well. At $55/month it's the most expensive option for a solo practitioner, but if you're planning to grow into a multi-room practice, it scales better than Halaxy.

**CorePlus** sits in the budget range with direct Medicare claiming and strong NDIS support. It's less polished than Cliniko or Nookal, but functional. The $5/month entry plan is limited; the $45 plan is more comparable to the others.

> [!key-takeaway]
> If you're starting solo and watching costs, start with Halaxy. It's free, it handles Medicare and NDIS, and it works. You can always switch later once you know what features matter to you. The best software is the one you'll actually use consistently, and "free" removes one barrier to that.

## Setting Up Medicare Claiming

Before you can bill Medicare CDM sessions, you need three things configured: a PRODA account, access to HPOS, and claiming set up in your practice management software.

### Step 1: PRODA account

PRODA (Provider Digital Access) is your identity verification with Services Australia. You need it to access any government health service portal.

Set this up early. Go to servicesaustralia.gov.au/proda, create an account, and verify your identity. You'll need your Medicare card, a photo ID, and a phone number. The verification process is usually instant but can take a few days if your identity check needs manual review.

### Step 2: HPOS access

Once PRODA is set up, link it to HPOS (Health Professional Online Services). This is where you apply for your provider number and where you can manually submit Medicare claims if needed.

You applied for your provider number through HPOS (see the appendix for the step-by-step). Once it's active, confirm you can log in and see your provider details.

### Provider numbers for exercise physiologists

If you're an EP, the provider number process has an extra layer. Most CDM professions are AHPRA-registered, which means their registration is the credential Services Australia checks when processing your provider number application. EPs aren't AHPRA-registered. You're accredited through ESSA (Exercise & Sports Science Australia), and you need to be an Accredited Exercise Physiologist (AEP) specifically, not just an accredited exercise scientist (AES).

**What you need before applying:**
- Current AEP accreditation through ESSA (not AES, the accreditation levels are different for Medicare purposes)
- Your ESSA accreditation certificate or member number
- Proof of your practice location (same as everyone else)
- PRODA account linked to HPOS (same as everyone else)

**The application process** is the same HW093 form through HPOS. Where AHPRA-registered practitioners list their AHPRA registration number, you provide your ESSA accreditation details. Processing times are the same (2-6 weeks).

**Maintaining your accreditation** matters more for EPs than AHPRA registration does for other professions, because if your ESSA accreditation lapses, your Medicare provider number becomes invalid. ESSA accreditation requires annual renewal and ongoing CPD. Set a calendar reminder well before your renewal date. Letting it lapse, even briefly, means you can't bill Medicare until it's reinstated.

> [!warning]
> **AES vs AEP matters.** An Accredited Exercise Scientist (AES) cannot get a Medicare provider number. Only Accredited Exercise Physiologists (AEP) qualify. If you're still working toward your AEP accreditation, you can see private patients and NDIS participants, but you can't bill Medicare CDM until the AEP is confirmed.

### Step 3: Configure your software

This is where most practitioners get stuck, not because it's hard, but because they put it off.

In your practice management software (Halaxy, Cliniko, whatever you chose), you need to enter your provider number, link your PRODA credentials for direct claiming, set up your item numbers (the common CDM ones are 10960 for individual sessions, 81110 for group sessions), and configure your standard session types with the correct fees and item numbers attached.

Each software has its own setup wizard for this. Halaxy walks you through it during onboarding. Cliniko requires a Tyro terminal or Easyclaim configuration. The process takes 20-30 minutes if you have your provider number and PRODA login ready.

### Step 4: Run a test claim

Before your first real CDM patient, run a test claim. Process a session, submit the claim, and confirm it goes through without errors. If something's misconfigured (wrong provider number format, incorrect item number, PRODA link not working), you want to find out now, not when a patient is waiting for their rebate.

> [!warning]
> **Don't skip the test claim.** The most common issue is a PRODA link that looks connected but isn't actually transmitting claims. A single test claim takes 5 minutes and saves you from discovering a billing problem after seeing 10 patients.

## Professional Indemnity Insurance

You can't see patients without it. For AHPRA-registered professions, it's a legal requirement. For everyone else, it's a practical one. No insurance policy means one complaint or incident could end your career and your finances.

### What you need

**Professional indemnity (PI) insurance** covers claims against you for professional negligence, errors in treatment, or adverse outcomes. This is the core policy. You need it.

**Public liability insurance** covers injuries to patients or visitors on your premises. Someone trips in your waiting room, equipment falls on a patient. This is usually a separate policy or an add-on.

### How much coverage

Most professional associations recommend a minimum of $5 million PI cover. Many recommend $10-20 million. The premium difference between $5M and $20M is often surprisingly small ($200-$500/year more), and if a claim ever reaches seven figures, you want to be well over the threshold rather than scraping under it.

### Where to get it

Your professional association is usually the best starting point. Most offer insurance through a negotiated group scheme that's cheaper than going direct to an insurer, and the coverage is tailored to your profession.

| Provider type | Typical cost | Notes |
|---------------|-------------|-------|
| Professional association scheme | $500–$1,500/yr | Usually cheapest, profession-specific cover |
| Specialist health insurance broker | $800–$2,000/yr | More flexibility on coverage |
| Direct insurer (BizCover, etc.) | $600–$2,500/yr | Easy online quotes, may miss profession-specific needs |

### What to check before you sign

Read the policy document, not just the summary. Specifically:

- **Scope of practice.** Does the policy cover everything you actually do? If you offer dry needling, clinical pilates, hydrotherapy, home visits, or telehealth, confirm each one is covered. "Professional indemnity insurance" doesn't automatically mean "covers everything you do."
- **Run-off cover.** If you stop practising or switch insurers, does the policy cover claims made after the policy ends for incidents that happened during it? You want this.
- **Excess.** What's your out-of-pocket per claim? A $0 excess is nice but costs more. A $500-$1,000 excess is standard and manageable.
- **Retroactive date.** From when does the cover start? For new practitioners this is usually straightforward, but check.

> [!tip]
> Get your insurance sorted at least 2 weeks before you see your first patient. Application processing takes a few days, and some insurers need to verify your registration. Don't let an insurance delay push back your start date.

## Telehealth

Telehealth is a standard part of most practices now. Setting it up costs almost nothing and gives your patients flexibility. Medicare CDM sessions attract the same rebate whether they're in-person or via telehealth, so there's no financial downside.

### Legal requirements

To bill Medicare for telehealth CDM sessions, the consultation must be via video (audio-only doesn't qualify for allied health CDM telehealth items), must meet the same 20-minute minimum as in-person sessions, and must be clinically appropriate. You need the patient's informed consent for telehealth, which can be verbal but should be noted in their record.

Your professional obligations (clinical notes, treatment plans, duty of care) are the same as in-person. Telehealth doesn't lower the bar on documentation.

### Software

You don't need specialist telehealth software. Most practice management systems have a built-in video option, and if yours doesn't, a standard video platform works.

**Built-in options:** Halaxy and Cliniko both offer integrated telehealth. The patient gets a link with their booking confirmation, clicks it at appointment time, and you're in a video call within your practice management system. Clinical notes, billing, and the consultation happen in the same place.

**External options:** If your software doesn't have built-in video, a separate platform like Coviu (designed for Australian healthcare, HIPAA-equivalent privacy) works. Some practitioners use standard video tools, but purpose-built health platforms handle consent and privacy requirements more cleanly.

### When it works

Follow-up appointments with established patients, exercise prescription reviews, treatment plan discussions, patient education, and consultations where the clinical assessment doesn't require hands-on examination. If you can deliver the clinical value without touching the patient, telehealth works.

### When it doesn't

Initial assessments where you need to physically examine someone. Manual therapy sessions. Any treatment that requires equipment. Patients who aren't comfortable with technology (common in older CDM demographics). Use judgement. If the patient would get a worse outcome via telehealth, see them in person.

> [!key-takeaway]
> Add telehealth as an option, not a replacement. Most CDM patients prefer in-person care. But for follow-ups, reviews, and patients who'd otherwise cancel due to transport or illness, a telehealth option keeps them in their treatment plan instead of dropping out. The Medicare rebate is the same either way.

## Equipment

Keep this minimal to start. Every dollar you spend on equipment before you have patients is a dollar that isn't in your buffer.

**Every profession needs:** a treatment table or desk (depending on your work), a laptop or desktop for your software, a phone, basic clinical consumables for your profession, and cleaning supplies for infection control.

**Profession-specific equipment** varies widely. A physio needs a treatment plinth, exercise equipment, and potentially electrotherapy devices. An EP needs exercise equipment and assessment tools. A dietitian needs very little beyond a desk and a computer. A podiatrist needs a treatment chair and instruments.

Start with the essentials for your profession and add as demand justifies it. You don't need a fully kitted clinic on day one. You need enough to see your first 20 patients well.
