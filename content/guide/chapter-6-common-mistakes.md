# Chapter 6: Common Mistakes & What They Cost

> [!tldr]
> Ten mistakes with real dollar figures. Every one is preventable, and preventing them costs a fraction of making them. The total cost of getting these wrong can easily hit $30,000-$50,000 in your first year. The total cost of avoiding them is about $3,000-$6,000.

> [!key-number]
> **$14,400/yr** — cost of underpricing by $15/session
> **$8K–$20K** — delayed income from skipping GP outreach
> **$5K–$15K** — first-year tax surprise when you haven't been saving

These aren't hypothetical. They're patterns that come up across professions, locations, and experience levels. Some of them are covered in more detail in other chapters — this is the "know the stakes" version.

## 1. Underpricing your sessions

**Costs you: $10,000–$15,000+ per year**

Covered in detail in Chapter 3, but the numbers bear repeating. If you set your fee $15 below the median for your area and see 20 patients per week for 48 weeks, that's $14,400 in revenue you didn't collect. Not gross revenue that would've been eaten by expenses — that's money that goes straight to your bottom line, because your costs are the same regardless of what you charge.

New practitioners underprice because a lower number feels safer to say out loud. The irony is that it doesn't attract more patients. CDM referrals come from GP relationships, not from being the cheapest option in the suburb. Set your fee at or slightly above the median and review Chapter 3 for the full walkthrough.

## 2. Not building GP relationships before opening

**Costs you: 2-4 months of delayed income ($8,000–$20,000)**

Some practitioners set up their practice, put up a website, and wait. Then they spend two months wondering why nobody's booked in.

CDM patients come through GP referrals. GPs refer to practitioners they know. If you haven't introduced yourself to local GPs before opening, you're starting from zero on day one when you could've had a pipeline already building in the background.

Start visiting clinics 4-6 weeks before you open. You don't need the practice fully set up. You just need to be able to say "I'm opening at [location] in [month], here are my details for CDM referrals." Chapter 4 walks through exactly how.

## 3. Choosing the wrong business structure

**Costs you: $2,000–$5,000 to restructure**

Starting as a sole trader is fine for most people in Year 1. It's simple and cheap. But some practitioners start as a sole trader without thinking about when a Pty Ltd makes more sense, then find themselves restructuring 12-18 months later — new ABN, new contracts, transferring assets, updating registrations, legal fees.

The fix is a single conversation with your accountant before you start. Not "what structure should I use forever," but "what should I start with and at what income level should I switch?" If you expect to net over $120,000-$150,000 within 2-3 years, starting as a Pty Ltd from day one might save you the restructuring later.

## 4. Not setting aside money for tax

**Costs you: $5,000–$15,000+ in a surprise tax bill**

When you were employed, tax was invisible. It was taken out before you saw the money. In private practice, the full amount lands in your account and it feels like income. A third of it isn't.

In your first year, the ATO probably hasn't set up PAYG instalments for you yet, so no tax is collected during the year. Your tax return produces a bill for the entire year at once. If you've been spending everything that came in, that bill is a crisis.

The prevention is free: a separate savings account, 25-30% of your net income transferred in every time you get paid. Chapter 5 has the detail.

## 5. Skipping insurance or going too cheap

**Costs you: potentially everything**

Professional indemnity insurance is mandatory for AHPRA-registered professions — seeing patients without it is illegal. But beyond the legal requirement, some practitioners go for the cheapest policy without reading the coverage limits, skip public liability insurance for their practice space, or don't check what their policy actually excludes.

The cost of proper insurance is $1,000-$2,500/year. The cost of a single uninsured claim can run $50,000-$500,000+. This one doesn't need a detailed analysis — get appropriate coverage (at least $5 million PI, many associations recommend $10-20 million), add public liability, read the exclusions, and make sure anything outside core scope (dry needling, clinical pilates, hydrotherapy) is explicitly covered.

> [!warning]
> Check your policy exclusions. "Professional indemnity insurance" doesn't automatically cover every service you provide. If you offer something outside your standard scope of practice, confirm it's covered before you deliver it. Finding out you're not covered at the point of a claim is the worst possible timing.

## 6. Sloppy record keeping

**Costs you: $2,000–$10,000+ in lost deductions and penalties**

Every receipt you don't keep is a deduction you can't claim at tax time. Over a year of $30 purchases, $50 subscriptions, and $150 equipment buys, the missed deductions add up quickly. And if the ATO audits you and you can't produce records, you lose the deductions entirely.

The common version of this: mixing personal and business expenses in one bank account, not keeping receipts for small purchases, and leaving everything until tax time when you try to reconstruct a year of transactions from bank statements.

It takes 15 minutes a week to photograph receipts and categorise transactions in your accounting software. It takes an entire painful day to do it once a year, and you'll miss things. See Chapter 5 for the system.

## 7. No cancellation policy (or not enforcing it)

**Costs you: $3,000–$8,000+/year**

A solo practitioner seeing 20 patients a week will get 2-4 cancellations per week. That's normal. What's not normal is absorbing those as lost revenue without any policy in place.

If your session fee is $120 and you lose 3 sessions per week to no-shows and late cancellations, that's $360/week or about $17,000/year. A cancellation policy — even one that only recovers half of those through a mix of deterrence and actual charges — puts $8,000+ back in your pocket.

The policy itself is simple: 24 or 48 hours' notice, full session fee charged for late cancellations, stated in your patient agreement and mentioned verbally at the first appointment. The hard part is enforcing it consistently. Not selectively, not "I'll let it slide this time." Consistently, for everyone, from day one. Patients respect policies that are communicated clearly upfront. The ones who don't were going to be difficult patients regardless.

## 8. Trying to do everything yourself

**Costs you: burnout and slower growth**

Solo practice means you're responsible for everything. It doesn't mean you should do everything.

Bookkeeping, website setup, legal documents, cleaning — these are all things that cost less to outsource than they cost you in time. If you earn $100/hour seeing patients and you spend an hour doing bookkeeping that a $50/hour bookkeeper could've handled, that hour cost you $50 in lost revenue.

The temptation to save money by doing everything is strong in the first year. But your time is finite and your highest-value activity is seeing patients and building GP relationships. A cleaner at $50/week, a bookkeeper at $150-$300/month, and a one-time investment in a good website free up hours that are better spent on the things that grow your practice.

> [!tip]
> Start with the two things that drain the most time and energy: bookkeeping and cleaning. Both are inexpensive to outsource and immediately give you back hours you can spend on patients or GP visits. Everything else can wait until Year 2.

## 9. No emergency fund

**Costs you: a financial crisis when anything goes wrong**

Things that can and do happen in solo practice: you get sick for two weeks (lost revenue: $4,000-$6,000), your room rental arrangement falls through and you need a new space urgently, a piece of equipment breaks, a pandemic disrupts your patient flow. Without a cash buffer, any of these becomes an emergency. With one, they're inconveniences.

Build a buffer of 2-3 months of business expenses ($6,000-$15,000 depending on your costs) in a separate savings account. It takes time to build, and that's fine — start setting aside $500/month from the beginning and it'll be there by the time you need it.

## 10. Not getting help early enough

**Costs you: months of avoidable mistakes**

A lot of practitioners struggle alone for 12-18 months before talking to a mentor, joining a peer group, or getting professional advice beyond their accountant. By then they've made several of the mistakes on this list and they're trying to dig out of holes that could've been avoided.

Find a mentor — someone who's been through the startup phase in your profession. Many professional associations have mentoring programs. Join or form a peer group with other solo practitioners in your area, even from different professions. You're all facing the same business challenges, and a monthly catch-up to compare notes is surprisingly useful.

Get professional advice early: accountant from day one, and consider a session with a practice management consultant if your association offers one. The cost of an hour of expert advice is a rounding error compared to the cost of learning everything by expensive trial and error.

## The Prevention vs Cure Table

| Mistake | Cost of making it | Cost of preventing it |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Underpricing | $10,000–$15,000/yr | $0 (set the right fee) |
| No GP outreach | $8,000–$20,000 delayed income | $0 + your time |
| Wrong structure | $2,000–$5,000 restructuring | $500–$1,000 (accountant advice) |
| No tax savings | $5,000–$15,000 surprise bill | $0 (set up the account) |
| Skipping insurance | $50,000–$500,000+ | $1,000–$2,500/yr |
| Poor records | $2,000–$10,000 lost deductions | $0–$300/month (bookkeeper) |
| No cancellation policy | $3,000–$8,000/yr | $0 (implement one) |

Total cost of prevention: roughly $3,000-$6,000 in Year 1. Total cost of making these mistakes: easily $30,000-$50,000+.
