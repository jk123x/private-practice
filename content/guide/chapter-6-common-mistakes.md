# Chapter 6: Common Mistakes & What They Cost

Every practitioner who goes into private practice makes mistakes. That's fine. What matters is whether you make the expensive ones or the cheap ones. This chapter covers the mistakes that cost real money, real time, or real momentum, with actual dollar figures so you can see what's at stake.

These aren't hypothetical. They're patterns that repeat across professions, locations, and experience levels.

## Mistake 1: Underpricing Your Sessions

**What it costs: $10,000-$15,000+ per year**

This is the most common mistake and the most expensive one that practitioners make voluntarily. It's covered in detail in Chapter 3, but the numbers are worth repeating here.

If you underprice by $15 per session compared to the market median, and you see 20 patients per week for 48 weeks, that's $14,400 in lost revenue per year. Not gross revenue that disappears into expenses. That's $14,400 that would have gone directly to your bottom line, because your costs are the same regardless of what you charge.

**Why it happens:** New practitioners feel uncomfortable charging market rates. They worry patients won't come, so they set fees low to reduce the barrier. The irony is that underpricing doesn't attract more patients. GP referrals come from relationships and communication, not from being the cheapest option in the area.

**How to avoid it:** Research 5-10 practices in your area. Set your fee at or slightly above the median. Review Chapter 3 for the full pricing framework.

## Mistake 2: Not Building GP Relationships Before Opening

**What it costs: 2-4 months of delayed income ($8,000-$20,000)**

Some practitioners set up their practice, put up a website, and wait for patients to find them. Then they spend 2-4 months wondering why their diary is empty.

CDM patients come from GP referrals. GPs refer to practitioners they know and trust. If you haven't introduced yourself to local GPs before opening, you start from zero on day one instead of having a pipeline building in the background.

**Why it happens:** Practitioners focus on the setup (room, software, website, insurance) and forget that none of it matters without patients. GP outreach feels like sales, and clinicians hate sales.

**How to avoid it:** Start visiting GP clinics 4-6 weeks before you open. You don't need your practice to be fully set up. You just need to be able to say "I'm opening a practice at [location] in [month]. Here are my details for when you have CDM patients who need [your profession]." Chapter 4 has the full playbook.

## Mistake 3: Choosing the Wrong Business Structure (and Having to Change It)

**What it costs: $2,000-$5,000 in restructuring fees**

Starting as a sole trader is fine for most new practitioners. It's simple, cheap, and appropriate for Year 1. But some practitioners set up as a sole trader and then need to restructure to a Pty Ltd company within 12-18 months because:
- Their income has grown beyond the point where sole trader tax treatment is optimal
- They want asset protection
- Their accountant advises that a company structure would save significant tax

The restructuring itself costs money: new ABN, new contracts, transferring assets, updating registrations, accountant and potentially legal fees.

**Why it happens:** Practitioners make the structure decision without professional advice, or they get advice that's too conservative ("just start as a sole trader") without understanding when the switch should happen.

**How to avoid it:** Talk to your accountant before you start. Not about what structure to use forever, but about what structure to start with and what triggers the switch to a company structure. If you expect to earn over $120,000-$150,000 in net income within 2-3 years, starting as a Pty Ltd from day one might save you the restructuring cost later. This is a conversation your accountant should initiate. If they don't, ask.

## Mistake 4: Not Setting Aside Money for Tax

**What it costs: $5,000-$15,000+ tax bill with no savings to cover it**

In your first year of private practice, you may not have PAYG instalments set up. This means no tax is deducted from your income during the year. When you lodge your tax return and get a bill for $15,000, you need to have the money.

Many first-year practitioners don't. They spend everything they earn, treating their gross revenue like take-home pay. Then the tax bill arrives and they're scrambling.

**Why it happens:** As an employee, tax was invisible. It was deducted before you ever saw the money. In private practice, the money hits your account in full. It feels like it's all yours. It isn't.

**How to avoid it:** Open a separate savings account. Transfer 25-30% of your net income into it every time you pay yourself. Don't touch it. When the tax bill comes, the money is sitting there. Chapter 5 covers this in detail.

## Mistake 5: Skipping Insurance or Underinsuring

**What it costs: Potentially catastrophic (claims can be $50,000-$500,000+)**

Professional indemnity insurance is mandatory for AHPRA-registered practitioners and effectively mandatory for everyone else. But some practitioners:
- Delay getting insurance before seeing patients (this is illegal for AHPRA-regulated professions)
- Choose the cheapest policy without understanding coverage limits
- Don't get public liability insurance for their practice space
- Don't understand their policy exclusions

**Why it happens:** Insurance feels like a cost without a benefit until you need it. Practitioners see it as a box-ticking exercise and go for the lowest premium.

**How to avoid it:** Get professional indemnity insurance before you see your first patient. Period. Aim for at least $5 million coverage (many associations recommend $10-$20 million). Add public liability insurance. Read the policy exclusions. If you're doing anything outside your core scope (dry needling, clinical pilates, exercise prescription outside your usual domain), make sure it's explicitly covered.

The cost of appropriate insurance is $1,000-$2,500 per year. The cost of a single uninsured claim can end your career.

## Mistake 6: Poor Record Keeping

**What it costs: $2,000-$10,000+ in lost deductions, penalties, and accountant time**

Good record keeping doesn't just keep the ATO happy. It directly affects how much tax you pay. Every receipt you don't keep is a deduction you can't claim. Over a year, that adds up.

Common record keeping failures:
- Mixing personal and business expenses in one bank account
- Not keeping receipts for small purchases ("it was only $30")
- Not categorising expenses correctly in your accounting software
- Waiting until tax time to enter 12 months of transactions

**Why it happens:** Record keeping is boring. When you're busy seeing patients and building a practice, entering receipts into Xero feels like the lowest priority task in the world.

**How to avoid it:**
1. Dedicate business bank account. Every business purchase goes through it. Every personal purchase does not.
2. Photograph receipts immediately. Use your accounting software's mobile app.
3. Spend 15 minutes per week categorising transactions. Not per month. Per week. Friday afternoon, before you finish for the week.

## Mistake 7: Ignoring Cancellation Policy

**What it costs: $3,000-$8,000+ per year in no-shows and late cancellations**

A solo practitioner seeing 20 patients per week will experience roughly 2-4 cancellations per week. Without a cancellation policy, those slots go unfilled and uncompensated.

If your session fee is $120 and you lose 3 sessions per week to no-shows or late cancellations without charging, that's $360/week or $17,280/year. Even if a cancellation policy only recovers half of those (through a mix of deterrence and actual charges), that's $8,640 back in your pocket.

**Why it happens:** New practitioners feel awkward enforcing a cancellation fee. They worry about upsetting patients. So they either don't have a policy, or they have one and never enforce it.

**How to avoid it:**
1. Have a clear cancellation policy from day one (48 hours or 24 hours notice, depending on your preference).
2. Communicate it at the first appointment and in your patient agreement.
3. Enforce it consistently. Not selectively. Everyone gets the same treatment.
4. Most patients respect a cancellation policy when it's communicated clearly upfront. The ones who don't respect it are the ones who would have been difficult patients anyway.

## Mistake 8: Trying to Do Everything Yourself

**What it costs: Burnout, slower growth, and often more money than outsourcing**

Solo practice doesn't mean you do everything alone. It means you're responsible for everything. There's a difference.

Common areas where practitioners try to save money by doing it themselves:
- Bookkeeping (get an accountant or bookkeeper, $150-$300/month)
- Website (a simple, professional site is worth paying for once)
- Legal documents (service agreements, patient consent forms, terms of service)
- Cleaning (it's tempting to save $50/week by cleaning your own clinic. Your time is worth more than that)

**Why it happens:** New practitioners are cost-conscious (understandably) and think doing everything themselves saves money. It doesn't. It costs time, and your time should be spent seeing patients and building referral relationships.

**How to avoid it:** Outsource anything that costs less than your effective hourly rate. If you earn $100/hour seeing patients and a bookkeeper costs $50/hour, every hour you spend on bookkeeping is costing you $50 in lost revenue. Outsource the bookkeeping, see an extra patient.

## Mistake 9: No Emergency Fund

**What it costs: Financial crisis when something goes wrong**

Things that can go wrong in a solo practice:
- You get sick and can't see patients for 2 weeks (lost revenue: $4,000-$6,000)
- Your practice management software gets hacked and you're down for a week
- Your room rental arrangement falls through and you need to find a new space urgently
- A piece of essential equipment breaks
- A global event (pandemic, natural disaster) disrupts your patient flow

Without an emergency fund, any of these events creates a financial crisis. With one, they're inconveniences.

**How to avoid it:** Build a cash buffer of 2-3 months of business expenses ($6,000-$15,000 depending on your cost base). Keep it in a separate savings account. Replenish it if you ever need to use it.

## Mistake 10: Not Getting Help Early Enough

**What it costs: Varies, but often the difference between a practice that thrives and one that barely survives**

Many practitioners struggle alone for 12-18 months before seeking help from a mentor, peer group, or business advisor. By that point, they've made several of the mistakes above and are trying to dig out of holes that could have been avoided.

**How to avoid it:**
- Find a mentor. Someone who's been through the private practice startup phase, ideally in your profession. Many professional associations have mentoring programs.
- Join or form a peer group. Other solo practitioners in your area (even from different professions) face the same business challenges. Meeting monthly to share experiences is valuable.
- Get professional advice early. Accountant from day one. A session with a practice management consultant can pay for itself many times over.

## The Cost of Getting It Right

Here's the interesting thing: all of the mistakes above are preventable, and preventing them costs less than making them.

| Mistake | Cost of Making It | Cost of Preventing It |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Underpricing | $10,000-$15,000/yr | $0 (just set the right fee) |
| No GP outreach | $8,000-$20,000 delayed income | $0 + your time |
| Wrong structure | $2,000-$5,000 restructuring | $500-$1,000 (accountant advice upfront) |
| No tax savings | $5,000-$15,000 surprise bill | $0 (just set up the account) |
| Skipping insurance | $50,000-$500,000+ claim | $1,000-$2,500/yr |
| Poor records | $2,000-$10,000 lost deductions | $0-$300/month (bookkeeper) |
| No cancellation policy | $3,000-$8,000/yr | $0 (just implement one) |

The total cost of prevention is roughly $3,000-$6,000 in your first year. The total cost of making these mistakes can easily exceed $30,000-$50,000. The maths is obvious.
