## Your Claiming Workflow

There are two ways to claim Medicare for CDM sessions. One of them is worth using. The other isn't.

**Direct billing through your software** means the claim is submitted electronically at the point of service. The patient's rebate is processed automatically and either goes to their bank account or offsets their payment (if you're bulk billing). You get confirmation that the claim went through before the patient leaves.

**Patient claims their own rebate** means you give the patient a receipt and they submit it themselves through Medicare Online or a Medicare office. This works, but it creates friction. Patients forget, delay, or don't know how. Some never claim at all and then resent paying the full fee because they associate you with the out-of-pocket cost rather than the gap.

> [!key-takeaway]
> Set up direct billing. Every practice management system that handles Medicare can do this. It takes 20-30 minutes to configure (covered in Chapter 2) and it means every CDM patient gets their rebate processed on the spot. Less admin for you, less friction for them, fewer "I'm paying too much" complaints.

### Checking referral validity

Before you bill a CDM session, you need a valid referral. The referral must come from a GP (not a specialist, not another allied health practitioner), the patient must have a current GPCCMP, and the referral must name your profession (it doesn't have to name you specifically, but it must specify the allied health discipline).

In practice, referrals arrive in various states of completeness. Sometimes it's a proper letter on the GP's letterhead. Sometimes it's a scribbled note faxed to your clinic. Sometimes the patient says "my GP said to come see you" and has no paperwork at all.

**If the patient has no referral documentation:** Don't see them under Medicare CDM. You can see them as a private patient, but you can't bill Medicare without a valid referral. Ask them to contact their GP to fax or email the referral, and book them in once you have it. Billing without a referral is the single most common audit trigger.

**If the referral is unclear:** Call the GP's clinic. Confirm there's a GPCCMP in place and that the referral is intended for your profession. A 2-minute phone call now saves a rejected claim later.

> [!tip]
> Create a simple system for tracking referrals. A column in your patient spreadsheet or a tag in your practice management software. For every CDM patient: do you have the referral on file? When was it dated? How many sessions has this patient had this calendar year? This information prevents billing errors before they happen.

## Managing the 5-Session Shared Cap

This is where CDM billing gets operationally tricky. Every patient with a GPCCMP gets 5 Medicare-subsidised allied health sessions per calendar year. Those 5 sessions are shared across ALL allied health providers, not 5 per provider.

If a patient sees a physio for 3 sessions, a podiatrist for 1, and then comes to you, they have 1 session remaining. Not 5.

### Why this matters for your practice

It affects treatment planning. You can't design a 6-session treatment plan for a CDM patient and assume Medicare will cover all 6. It might only cover 2, depending on how many sessions they've used elsewhere.

It affects patient expectations. If you tell a patient "Medicare covers 5 sessions" without checking their usage, they'll be surprised and frustrated when you tell them at session 3 that their allocation is used up. That frustration lands on you, even though it's the system's limitation.

It affects your revenue. When a CDM patient's Medicare sessions run out, they either switch to private fees (higher out-of-pocket) or stop coming. Knowing this in advance lets you plan the conversation rather than reacting to it.

### How to check remaining sessions

**Ask the patient.** "Have you seen any other allied health practitioners this year under Medicare?" Most patients know, roughly. If they've seen a physio, a podiatrist, and a dietitian, they've probably used 3+ sessions already.

**Check with the GP.** The GP's software usually tracks how many CDM referral sessions have been used. A quick call to the practice manager can confirm.

**Your software may show it.** Some practice management systems pull Medicare claiming history and show remaining sessions for a patient. Check whether yours does.

The best approach: ask at the first appointment, note it in the patient record, and plan your treatment accordingly.

### When sessions run out

This is the conversation nobody prepares for, and it happens regularly. A patient is making progress, has 2 sessions left, and needs more treatment. Your options:

**Continue as private patients.** The patient pays your full fee with no Medicare rebate. Some patients are fine with this, especially if they have private health insurance that covers some of the gap. Explain it clearly: "Your Medicare sessions for the year are used up. We can continue as private appointments at $[fee], and if you have health insurance extras, you can claim part of that back."

**Space out remaining sessions.** If the patient has 2 sessions left and it's April, you might stretch them across the remaining year with longer gaps between appointments. This works for maintenance-type care, not acute treatment.

**Wait for the calendar year to reset.** The 5-session cap resets on 1 January. If it's October and the patient has 0 sessions remaining, waiting 2-3 months might make more sense than 3 private sessions at full gap. Discuss this with the patient.

**Explore other funding.** NDIS, DVA (Department of Veterans' Affairs), workers compensation, private health insurance extras. Some patients have alternative funding sources they haven't thought to use for allied health.

> [!key-takeaway]
> Know how many sessions a patient has left BEFORE you design their treatment plan. The conversation about running out of Medicare sessions is much easier when you've discussed it upfront ("You have 3 sessions covered by Medicare this year, and we'll review after that") than when it's a surprise at session 4.

## Common Billing Errors and What They Cost

These are the errors that either get claims rejected or put you on the wrong end of a Medicare audit. Each one is preventable with basic systems.

### Billing without a valid referral

**What happens:** Claim gets rejected, or if it processes and gets audited later, you repay the rebate plus potential penalties.

**What it costs:** $56.70 per incorrectly claimed session. If you're routinely billing without checking referrals and it gets flagged in an audit, the repayment across dozens of sessions adds up fast. Plus the audit itself is stressful and time-consuming.

**Prevention:** Don't bill a CDM session until you have a referral document on file. No referral, no Medicare claim. See the patient as a private patient if they want to proceed without one.

### Using the wrong item number

**What happens:** Claim gets rejected or underpaid. Different professions use different item numbers, and using the wrong one for your profession or session type is a common mistake.

**What it costs:** Rejected claims mean delayed payment and time spent resubmitting. If the wrong item number results in a higher rebate than you're entitled to, that's an overpayment you'll need to repay.

**Prevention:** Set up your practice management software with the correct item numbers for your profession and session types. The common ones for CDM allied health are 10960 (individual, 20+ min), 81110 (group, 60+ min). Confirm which items apply to your specific profession during your software setup.

### Not meeting the 20-minute minimum

**What happens:** Medicare CDM sessions must include a minimum of 20 minutes of face-to-face clinical care. If you're running 15-minute follow-ups and billing them as CDM sessions, those claims aren't valid.

**What it costs:** Every claim for a sub-20-minute session is technically incorrect. If audited, you'd repay the rebate for each one. Running 15-minute sessions 4 times a day, 5 days a week, for 48 weeks is 960 potentially invalid claims. At $56.70 each. Do the maths.

**Prevention:** Schedule CDM sessions for at least 30 minutes. That gives you 20+ minutes of face-to-face time plus changeover. If you're running shorter sessions, bill them as private, not CDM.

### Billing after the cap is reached

**What happens:** You claim a 6th (or 7th, or 8th) Medicare-subsidised session for a patient who's already used their 5. The claim may process initially because Medicare's cross-provider tracking isn't always real-time, but it'll be flagged eventually.

**What it costs:** You repay the rebate for every session billed over the cap. If you've been telling patients they have more sessions than they actually do, you've also set a wrong expectation.

**Prevention:** Track session numbers in your software. Ask patients about prior allied health visits at the first appointment. Check with the GP if you're unsure.

> [!warning]
> Medicare compliance audits are real. They're data-driven and target patterns: unusually high claim volumes, repeated billing near the session cap, claims without matching referrals on file. Most practitioners will never be audited, but having clean records and proper documentation is the only protection if you are. The penalties for systematic errors are significant: repayment of all incorrectly claimed amounts, potential fines, and in serious cases, provider number suspension.

## Talking to Patients About Medicare

Your patients don't understand Medicare CDM. That's not a criticism. The system is genuinely confusing, and most people have never encountered it before their GP tells them they're eligible for subsidised allied health sessions.

The conversation you have at the first appointment sets the tone for the entire treatment relationship. Get it right and the patient understands what they're paying, what Medicare covers, and when they'll run out. Get it wrong and you'll spend the next 3 months fielding confused questions and managing frustration.

### At the first appointment

Cover three things, briefly, before you start the clinical work:

**What Medicare covers.** "Your GP has referred you under the Medicare chronic disease management program. That means Medicare covers part of the cost of up to 5 sessions per calendar year. The rebate is about $57 per session, and the difference between that and our fee is your out-of-pocket cost."

**What the gap is.** "Our session fee is $[X]. After the Medicare rebate, your out-of-pocket is about $[gap]. If you have private health insurance extras, you may be able to claim some of that back as well."

**What happens after 5 sessions.** "The 5-session limit is per calendar year and shared across all allied health providers, so if you've seen other allied health professionals this year, some of those sessions may already be used. Once the Medicare sessions are used, we can continue on a private basis at the same fee, just without the rebate."

That's it. Three sentences covering the key facts. Patients absorb this much better when you tell them upfront than when they discover it piecemeal.

### The "how many sessions will I need" conversation

Patients ask this at the first appointment. They're really asking two things: how long will treatment take, and how much will it cost?

Be honest about both. "Based on what I've found today, I'd recommend [X] sessions over [Y] weeks. Medicare covers up to 5 sessions this calendar year, and you've used [N] of those. So Medicare will cover [remaining] sessions, and after that we'd continue at the private rate if you want to keep going."

Patients respect honesty about this. What they don't respect is finding out later that their sessions have run out and nobody mentioned it.

### When a patient runs out and you need to tell them

"Your Medicare sessions for this year are used up. We can continue your treatment as private appointments. The session fee stays the same at $[X], but without the Medicare rebate, your out-of-pocket is the full amount. If you have health insurance extras, you can claim through that. Would you like to continue, or would you prefer to wait until your Medicare sessions reset in January?"

No apology. No overexplanation. State the facts, give them options, let them decide.

## Private Health Insurance Rebates

Medicare CDM isn't the only way patients get money back. Many patients have private health insurance with extras cover that includes allied health. This is separate from Medicare and applies whether the patient is seeing you under CDM or as a private patient.

**How it works for you:** You don't need to do anything special to enable private health claiming. If you have a HICAPS terminal (or equivalent), the patient can claim their insurance rebate on the spot by tapping their health fund card. If you don't have HICAPS, they claim online through their fund after the appointment.

**How much patients get back:** Varies wildly by fund, plan level, and profession. Could be $15 or $60 per session. You can't predict it and you shouldn't try. Just let patients know: "If you have private health extras, you can claim part of your gap through your fund as well."

**Should you get HICAPS?** A HICAPS terminal lets patients claim private health rebates at the point of service (same tap-and-go process as in a GP clinic). It costs around $30-$50/month plus a small per-transaction fee. For patient convenience, it's worth considering once your patient volume justifies the monthly cost. In the first few months, patients can claim from their fund directly. It's one less cost to carry before you need it.

> [!key-takeaway]
> A CDM patient with private health extras can claim BOTH Medicare rebate AND their health fund rebate for the same session. Medicare covers part of the gap, and their health fund may cover more. For some patients, the out-of-pocket after both rebates is very small. Mentioning this at the first appointment removes a lot of the price anxiety.
