# Chapter 8: Common Mistakes & What They Cost

> [!tldr]
> Ten mistakes with real dollar figures attached. Every one is preventable, and preventing them costs a fraction of making them. These aren't the obvious ones (underprice, don't visit GPs). These are the ones that catch people who thought they'd covered their bases.

> [!key-number]
> **$2,700+/yr** cost of not understanding the session cap
> **$5,000–$15,000** cost of signing a lease too early
> **$3,000–$8,000/yr** revenue lost to cancellations without a policy

These aren't hypothetical. They're patterns that come up across professions, locations, and experience levels. The other chapters cover pricing, GP outreach, tax, and bookkeeping in detail. This chapter covers the mistakes that don't fit neatly into those topics.

## 1. Not understanding the 5-session shared cap

**Costs you: $2,700+ in rebilled sessions and patient trust**

You design a 6-session treatment plan for a CDM patient. At session 4, you discover they've already used 2 sessions with another allied health provider this calendar year. They've hit the cap. The remaining sessions either become private (surprising the patient with a higher out-of-pocket) or you absorb the loss.

The cap is per calendar year, shared across ALL allied health providers. Not per provider. A patient who sees a physio, a podiatrist, and a dietitian gets 5 sessions total between all three of them.

The fix: ask every CDM patient at their first appointment how many allied health sessions they've had this year. Note it. Plan your treatment around the number that's actually available. Chapter 3 covers how to manage this operationally.

## 2. Signing a lease instead of renting rooms

**Costs you: $5,000-$15,000+ in unnecessary fixed costs (or worse, a lease you can't exit)**

Commercial leases are 3-5 years with personal guarantees. If your practice doesn't build as fast as expected, you're still paying $2,000-$4,000/month in rent plus outgoings. If you need to exit early, the break fees can be several months' rent.

Room rental in an existing clinic costs $200-$600/week, usually on a month-to-month or short-term agreement. If the location doesn't work, you leave.

Every dollar you commit to fixed overhead before you have the patient volume to support it is a dollar of pressure on your business. Room rental keeps your fixed costs proportional to your actual revenue. A lease locks them in whether patients show up or not.

**The exception:** If you've been renting rooms for 18+ months, your patient load is consistently full, and you want to expand or control your own space, a lease starts to make sense. Not before.

## 3. Choosing the wrong location

**Costs you: 6-12 months of slow growth and potentially starting over**

Location affects everything. Your proximity to GPs determines your referral pipeline. Your local demographics determine how many CDM patients exist in your area. Competition determines how many of them come to you.

The common version of this mistake: choosing a location because the rent is cheap, because it's near your home, or because a room happened to be available. None of those are bad factors, but none of them are the right primary factor.

Check the area's demographics (median age, chronic disease prevalence), map the competition (how many practitioners in your profession within 5-10km), count the GP clinics nearby, and visit the location during business hours to assess parking and accessibility. Chapter 2 covers the specifics.

Changing locations after you've started isn't catastrophic, but it means a new provider number (4-6 weeks processing), re-introducing yourself to a new set of GPs, and rebuilding local visibility from scratch. Get it right the first time.

## 4. Not setting up telehealth

**Costs you: 2-4 lost appointments per week ($10,000-$20,000/yr)**

Telehealth isn't about replacing in-person care. It's about keeping patients in their treatment plan when they'd otherwise cancel.

The patient who's feeling sick but not that sick. The one whose car is in the shop. The one who's 30 minutes away and it's raining. Without telehealth, those are all cancellations. With telehealth, they're appointments. At $120 per session, preventing even 2 cancellations per week adds up to $11,520 over a year.

Medicare CDM sessions attract the same rebate for telehealth as in-person. Your practice management software likely has built-in video capability. Setting it up takes under an hour and costs nothing. The only reason not to offer it is if you haven't got around to it. Chapter 2 walks through the setup.

## 5. Ignoring private health insurance claiming

**Costs you: patient satisfaction and retention**

A lot of new practitioners focus entirely on Medicare CDM and forget that many of their patients also have private health insurance extras. Those extras cover part of the gap fee for allied health sessions. For some patients, the difference between "I pay $63 out of pocket" and "I pay $25 out of pocket" is the difference between completing their treatment plan and dropping out after 3 sessions.

You don't need to understand every health fund's schedule. You just need to mention it. "If you have private health extras, you can claim part of your gap back through your fund." And eventually, consider getting a HICAPS terminal so patients can claim on the spot.

The mistake isn't financial for you directly. It's about patient retention. Patients who feel the cost is manageable keep coming back. Patients who feel it's expensive look for cheaper options. Chapter 3 covers how the rebate stacking works.

## 6. Not having a website with fees displayed

**Costs you: patients who never book**

When someone gets referred to you by their GP, the first thing they do is Google your name. If they can't find a website, or they find one that says "call for pricing," a large proportion of them will book with someone who gives them the information upfront.

Your website doesn't need to be fancy. It needs: who you are, where you are, what you treat, your fees (all of them), how to book, and your contact details. That's it. You can build this in an afternoon with any website builder.

The fees part is important. "Call for pricing" feels evasive in 2026. People want to know what they're up for before they call. Displaying your fees also means the patients who DO book have already accepted your price. One less awkward conversation.

## 7. Skipping insurance or going too cheap

**Costs you: potentially everything**

Professional indemnity insurance is mandatory for AHPRA-registered professions. Seeing patients without it is illegal. But beyond the legal requirement, some practitioners go for the cheapest policy without reading the coverage limits, skip public liability insurance for their practice space, or don't check what their policy excludes.

The cost of proper insurance is $1,000-$2,500/year. The cost of a single uninsured claim can run $50,000-$500,000+. Get appropriate coverage (at least $5 million PI, many associations recommend $10-20 million), add public liability, read the exclusions, and make sure anything outside core scope (dry needling, clinical pilates, hydrotherapy) is explicitly covered.

> [!warning]
> Check your policy exclusions. "Professional indemnity insurance" doesn't automatically cover every service you provide. If you offer something outside your standard scope of practice, confirm it's covered before you deliver it. Finding out you're not covered at the point of a claim is the worst possible timing.

## 8. Trying to do everything yourself

**Costs you: burnout and slower growth**

Solo practice means you're responsible for everything. It doesn't mean you should do everything.

Bookkeeping, website setup, legal documents, cleaning. These all cost less to outsource than they cost you in time. If you earn $100/hour seeing patients and spend an hour doing bookkeeping that a $50/hour bookkeeper could handle, that hour cost you $50 in lost revenue.

Your time is finite and your highest-value activity is seeing patients and building GP relationships. A cleaner at $50/week, a bookkeeper at $150-$300/month, and a one-time investment in a good website free up hours for the things that actually grow your practice.

> [!tip]
> Start with bookkeeping and cleaning. Both are inexpensive to outsource and immediately give you back hours you can spend on patients or GP visits. Everything else can wait until Year 2.

## 9. Setting up as Pty Ltd too early (or too late)

**Costs you: $2,000-$5,000 either way**

Too early: you pay for the company setup ($500-$1,500), annual ASIC fees ($300+), a more expensive accountant ($3,000-$6,000/year vs $1,500-$4,000), and company tax compliance, all while your income is $50,000 and the structure provides no tax benefit.

Too late: you've been running as a sole trader for three years, your income is $160,000, and you've been paying marginal tax rates up to 45% on income that would've been taxed at 25% through a company. Restructuring now costs $2,000-$5,000 in legal and accounting fees, plus the hassle of new ABN, new contracts, transferring assets, and updating every registration.

The fix is a single conversation with your accountant before you start. Ask: "Should I start as a sole trader or Pty Ltd, and at what income level should I switch?" Most accountants recommend starting as a sole trader and switching when net income consistently exceeds $120,000-$150,000. But your specific situation might be different, and a 30-minute conversation is cheaper than getting it wrong.

## 10. Not getting help early enough

**Costs you: months of avoidable mistakes**

A lot of practitioners go it alone for 12-18 months before talking to a mentor, joining a peer group, or getting professional advice beyond their accountant. By then they've made several of the mistakes on this list and they're digging out of holes that didn't need to exist.

Find a mentor who's been through the startup phase in your profession. Many professional associations have mentoring programs. Join or form a peer group with other solo practitioners in your area, even from different professions. You're all facing the same business challenges, and a monthly catch-up to compare notes is surprisingly useful.

Get professional advice early: accountant from day one, and consider a session with a practice management consultant if your association offers one. The cost of an hour of expert advice is a rounding error compared to learning everything through expensive trial and error.

## The Prevention vs Cure Table

| Mistake | Cost of making it | Cost of preventing it |
|---------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Session cap surprise | $2,700+/yr | $0 (ask the question) |
| Lease too early | $5,000-$15,000+ | $0 (rent rooms instead) |
| Wrong location | 6-12 months slow growth | $0 + a few hours research |
| No telehealth | $10,000-$20,000/yr lost | $0 (set it up in an hour) |
| No HICAPS/health fund info | Lost patient retention | $30-$50/month (HICAPS) or $0 (mention it) |
| No website with fees | Lost bookings | $0-$30/month (basic site) |
| Skipping insurance | $50,000-$500,000+ | $1,000-$2,500/yr |
| Everything yourself | Burnout, slow growth | $200-$500/month (outsource basics) |
| Wrong structure timing | $2,000-$5,000 | $500-$1,000 (accountant advice) |
| No mentor/peer group | Months of avoidable errors | $0 (join your association) |

Total cost of prevention: roughly $3,000-$6,000 in Year 1. Total cost of making these mistakes: easily $30,000-$50,000+.
