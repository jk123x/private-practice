# Chapter 2: NDIS Basics for CDM Practitioners

> [!tldr]
> You probably don't need to register as an NDIS provider when you're starting out. Most NDIS participants (60-70%) are plan-managed or self-managed and can see you whether you're registered or not. Registration costs $2K-5K in audit fees and adds real compliance overhead. Start unregistered, register later if your NDIS caseload grows enough to justify it.

> [!key-number]
> **$2,000–$5,000** — upfront audit cost to register as an NDIS provider
> **60–70%** — NDIS participants who can see unregistered providers
> **7–14 days** — typical payment time from plan managers

NDIS participants will walk through your door whether you plan for them or not. For some professions — exercise physiology, OT, speech pathology — they could make up 30-50% of your caseload. For physios and podiatrists, the proportion is smaller but growing.

The question everyone asks first is: "Do I need to register as an NDIS provider?" For most new solo practitioners, the answer is no. But you still need to understand how the system works so you can see NDIS participants properly and get paid without headaches.

## Registered vs Unregistered

The distinction is simpler than it sounds. Registering means going through an audit with the NDIS Quality and Safeguards Commission. It costs money, takes time, and comes with ongoing compliance requirements. Not registering means you skip all of that but can't see one category of NDIS participant.

**If you register**, you can see everyone — including agency-managed participants, where the NDIA controls the funding directly. You get listed on the official provider finder. You also get price caps (the NDIS sets maximum rates for registered providers), ongoing compliance paperwork, and renewal audits every 2-3 years.

**If you don't register**, you can still see plan-managed and self-managed participants. That's the majority — roughly 60-70% of all NDIS participants. No audit fees, no compliance burden beyond your normal professional obligations, and more flexibility on pricing. You miss out on agency-managed participants (the remaining 30-40%) and the official directory listing.

> [!key-takeaway]
> For most solo practitioners starting out, staying unregistered is the practical choice. The audit costs ($2,000-5,000) are significant when you're already spending on startup. The compliance burden is real. And the majority of NDIS participants can see you regardless. You can always register later once you know how much NDIS work you're actually doing.

The exception: if your profession sees high volumes of NDIS participants (OT, speech path, and EP in particular) and you expect NDIS to be a major part of your revenue from day one, the upfront investment might pay for itself quickly through access to agency-managed participants. Talk to other practitioners in your area about what their NDIS mix looks like before deciding.

## How You Get Paid

Every NDIS participant has their plan managed in one of three ways. This determines who you invoice and how long you wait for the money.

**Agency-managed** — the NDIA controls the funds directly. You submit claims through the myplace provider portal, and payment usually comes through in 3-5 business days. You need to be a registered provider to see these participants.

**Plan-managed** — a plan management company handles the funding. You invoice the plan manager, they pay you, they sort it out with the NDIA. Payment times vary — typically 7-14 business days, though some plan managers are faster. This is the most common arrangement and you don't need to be registered.

**Self-managed** — the participant manages their own budget. You invoice them directly, they pay you, they claim reimbursement from the NDIA. From your end, this works exactly like any private patient.

> [!tip]
> Plan-managed payment delays (7-14 days) can create cash flow gaps if you're seeing a lot of NDIS participants. Track your outstanding NDIS invoices separately and factor the lag into your cash flow planning. If a plan manager is consistently slow (20+ days), follow up — some are better than others.

## NDIS Pricing

The NDIS publishes a price guide with maximum rates for different service types. A few things to know about how this works in practice.

Registered providers must charge at or below these rates. You can't exceed the published maximum, even if your standard private fee is higher. Unregistered providers aren't technically bound by the price guide, but most plan managers apply it anyway, so in practice you're usually working within the same limits.

The good news: NDIS rates for allied health are comparable to or slightly above standard private fees for most professions. They're not unreasonably low.

The other good news: unlike Medicare CDM, you can claim for non-face-to-face time. Report writing, phone calls with support coordinators, travel to home visits — these are all billable under NDIS. With Medicare, you only get paid for the session itself. This makes NDIS work comparatively well-compensated once you factor in the admin time.

## Getting Your Invoices Right

NDIS invoices need specific information or they'll bounce. Missing any of these delays your payment:

- Your ABN
- Participant's name and NDIS number
- Date of service
- Support item number (from the NDIS support catalogue — your practice management software should have these built in)
- Description of the service
- Duration
- Rate charged
- GST status

Most practice management software (Halaxy, Cliniko, etc.) has NDIS invoicing templates. Use them. Manually formatting NDIS invoices is tedious and error-prone, and it's the kind of thing that costs you hours you could've saved with a 5-minute software setup.

> [!warning]
> **Set up your software for NDIS invoicing before your first NDIS participant arrives.** It's easy to do in advance and awkward to figure out in the moment. Fumbling with invoice formats when a patient is waiting doesn't inspire confidence.

## The GST Trap

This catches people. The GST rules for NDIS services differ depending on your profession, and getting it wrong means either overcharging participants or owing the ATO money you never collected.

The general rule: medical services provided by AHPRA-registered health practitioners are GST-free. If you're a physio, podiatrist, OT, dietitian, speech pathologist, chiro, or osteo, your clinical services are typically GST-free.

The complication: exercise physiologists aren't AHPRA-registered. Their services may not qualify for the same GST exemption, which means an EP might need to charge GST on NDIS services. This affects your pricing, your invoicing, and your BAS.

> [!warning]
> If you're not AHPRA-registered (EPs, some counsellors), get specific GST advice from your accountant before seeing NDIS participants. This is one of those areas where guessing can cost you. The ATO has guidelines but the application varies by profession, and your accountant needs to confirm which rules apply to you.

## Things That Confuse Everyone

**"Can I bill NDIS and Medicare for the same person?"**

Not for the same service. If a participant has NDIS funding for allied health in their plan, those services should go through NDIS, not Medicare CDM. But there are situations where a participant uses NDIS for one type of service and Medicare for another (e.g., NDIS for exercise physiology, Medicare CDM for podiatry). The key principle is no double-dipping — one funding source per service type.

**"What about cancellations?"**

The NDIS has specific cancellation rules for registered providers: you can charge the full amount for cancellations with less than 2 clear business days' notice, as long as the participant's plan has capacity for the charge. For unregistered providers seeing plan-managed or self-managed participants, your cancellation policy is whatever you agree to in your service agreement. Most practitioners align with the NDIS 2-business-day rule for consistency.

**"Do I need a service agreement?"**

Yes. For every NDIS participant, you should have a written service agreement covering what you'll provide, how often, the cost, your cancellation policy, and how either party can end the arrangement. Your practice management software probably has a template. If not, the NDIS website has sample agreements you can adapt.

**"How do I find NDIS participants?"**

You don't need to market specifically for them. If you're building a practice in your area, they'll find you — through support coordinators who keep lists of local providers, plan managers who recommend people, word of mouth, and online directories like Clickability. The most effective thing you can do is build relationships with 2-3 local support coordinators. They're the people connecting participants with providers, and a good relationship with a support coordinator can be a steady referral source.

## NDIS as a Revenue Stream

NDIS adds complexity to your practice, but it also adds revenue that's independent of Medicare CDM. There's no 5-session annual cap like Medicare has. Participants who find a provider they trust tend to stay long-term. And the ability to bill for non-face-to-face work means your effective hourly rate on NDIS work can be higher than it looks at first glance.

It's worth treating NDIS as a deliberate part of your practice model rather than something that just happens to you.
