# Chapter 2: NDIS Basics for CDM Practitioners

If you're going into private practice as an allied health professional in Australia, you'll encounter NDIS participants. It's not a question of if, but how many and in what form. For some professions (exercise physiology, occupational therapy, speech pathology), NDIS work can make up 30-50% of your caseload. For others (physiotherapy, podiatry), it's smaller but growing.

This chapter covers what you need to know about NDIS from a business perspective. Not the clinical side. The business decisions: should you become a registered provider, how does billing work, what does plan management mean for you, and where do people get confused.

## NDIS Registered vs Unregistered: The Decision

This is the first question every practitioner asks, and the answer is less straightforward than it seems.

### Registered provider

To become an NDIS registered provider, you go through an audit process via the NDIS Quality and Safeguards Commission. The process involves:

- An application to the NDIS Commission
- An audit by an approved auditor (this costs money, typically $2,000-$5,000 for a solo practitioner verification audit)
- Meeting practice standards and a code of conduct
- Ongoing compliance requirements (incident reporting, complaints management, worker screening)

**What registration gives you:**
- You can provide services to ALL NDIS participants, including those who are agency-managed (where the NDIA manages their plan directly)
- Some participants and support coordinators prefer registered providers
- You're listed on the NDIS provider finder tool

**What registration costs you:**
- The audit fee upfront ($2,000-$5,000)
- Ongoing compliance time (documentation, incident reporting, annual declarations)
- Renewal audits every 2-3 years
- Restrictions on how you can charge (NDIS sets maximum prices for registered providers)

### Unregistered provider

You can provide NDIS-funded services without registration, but only to participants who are plan-managed or self-managed. You cannot see agency-managed participants.

**What unregistered gets you:**
- No audit costs
- No compliance burden beyond your standard professional obligations
- More flexibility on pricing (you're not bound by the NDIS price guide, though plan managers may apply it)
- Faster to set up (no waiting for audit and registration)

**What you miss:**
- Agency-managed participants (approximately 30-40% of the NDIS market)
- Being listed on the official provider finder

### So which should you choose?

For most solo practitioners starting out, **staying unregistered is the pragmatic choice**. Here's why.

The audit costs are significant when you're already spending on startup. The compliance burden is real and ongoing. And the majority of NDIS participants (60-70%) are plan-managed or self-managed, meaning they can see you regardless of your registration status.

The exception is if your profession sees a high volume of NDIS participants (OT, speech pathology, exercise physiology in particular) and you expect NDIS to be a major revenue stream. In that case, the upfront investment in registration may pay for itself quickly through access to agency-managed participants.

This matters because the decision isn't permanent. You can start unregistered and register later once you've established your practice and have a better sense of your NDIS caseload. Going the other way (registered to unregistered) is also possible, though less common.

## How NDIS Billing Works

NDIS billing is different from Medicare CDM billing in several important ways. Understanding these differences will save you confusion and cashflow problems.

### The three plan management types

Every NDIS participant has their plan managed in one of three ways. This determines how you get paid.

**1. Agency-managed**
The NDIA manages the participant's funding directly. You must be a registered provider to see these participants. You submit claims through the NDIS myplace provider portal. Payment typically takes 3-5 business days.

**2. Plan-managed**
A plan management company handles the participant's funding on their behalf. You invoice the plan manager directly. They pay you, and then claim from the NDIA. Payment times vary by plan manager (typically 7-14 business days, though some are faster).

**3. Self-managed**
The participant manages their own funding. You invoice them directly, they pay you, and they claim reimbursement from the NDIA. From your perspective, this works like any private patient: you provide the service, send an invoice, get paid.

### NDIS pricing

The NDIS publishes a price guide and support catalogue that sets maximum prices for different service types. These prices are updated periodically.

Key things to know about NDIS pricing:

- **Registered providers must charge at or below the NDIS price guide rates.** You can't charge above the maximum, even if your standard rate is higher.
- **Unregistered providers are technically not bound by the price guide.** However, most plan managers will only pay up to the price guide rate, so in practice, you're often limited to the same maximums.
- **NDIS rates for allied health are generally comparable to or slightly above standard private fees.** For most CDM practitioners, the rates are reasonable.
- **You can claim for non-face-to-face time.** Report writing, phone calls with support coordinators, travel time to home visits. This is different from Medicare, where you're only paid for the session itself.

### Invoicing requirements

NDIS invoices need specific information. Missing any of these will delay payment:

- Your ABN
- The participant's name and NDIS number
- Date of service
- Support item number (from the NDIS support catalogue)
- Description of the service provided
- Duration of the service
- Rate charged
- GST information (more on this below)

Most practice management software (Halaxy, Cliniko, etc.) has NDIS invoicing templates built in. Use them. Manual invoicing is error-prone and slow.

## The GST Question

GST and NDIS is an area where practitioners regularly get confused, and the rules differ depending on your profession.

**The general rule:** Medical services provided by registered health practitioners are GST-free. This covers AHPRA-registered professions (physio, podiatry, OT, etc.).

**The complication for non-AHPRA professions:** Exercise physiologists, for example, are not AHPRA-registered. Their services may not qualify for the GST exemption under the same rules. This means an EP might need to charge GST on NDIS services, which affects pricing and invoicing.

**What to do:** Get specific advice from your accountant about your profession and your specific services. This is not an area to guess on. The ATO has guidelines, but the application varies by profession, and getting it wrong means either overcharging participants or owing the ATO money you didn't collect.

## Common NDIS Confusion Points

### "Can I bill NDIS and Medicare for the same participant?"

Generally, no. A participant shouldn't be receiving the same service funded by both NDIS and Medicare at the same time. If a participant has NDIS funding for allied health services in their plan, those services should be funded through NDIS, not Medicare CDM.

However, there can be situations where a participant has NDIS funding for some services and Medicare funding for others. For example, a participant might have NDIS funding for exercise physiology but use Medicare CDM referrals for podiatry. The key principle is no double-dipping for the same service.

### "What about cancellation policies?"

NDIS has specific rules about cancellations. For registered providers:
- You can charge the full amount for a cancellation with less than 2 clear business days' notice (the "short notice cancellation" rule)
- The participant's plan must have capacity for the charge
- You need to have a cancellation policy in your service agreement

For unregistered providers seeing plan-managed or self-managed participants, your cancellation policy is whatever you and the participant agree to in your service agreement. Most practitioners align with the NDIS short notice cancellation rules for consistency.

### "Do I need a service agreement?"

Yes. For every NDIS participant, you should have a service agreement that covers:
- What services you'll provide
- How often
- The cost
- Your cancellation policy
- How to end the agreement

This protects both you and the participant. Most practice management software has service agreement templates, or you can find templates on the NDIS website.

### "How do I find NDIS participants?"

You don't need to market specifically for NDIS participants. If you're building a private practice in your area, NDIS participants will find you through:
- Support coordinators who maintain lists of local providers
- Plan managers who recommend providers to their clients
- Word of mouth from other participants
- Online directories (Clickability, Disability Support Guide, your own website)

The most effective channel is building relationships with local support coordinators. They're the people who help participants find and connect with providers. Introduce yourself, explain your services, and make it easy for them to refer to you.

## Practical Recommendations for New Practitioners

1. **Start unregistered** unless NDIS work will be a major part of your caseload from day one. You can always register later.

2. **Set up your software for NDIS invoicing** from the start. Even if you don't have NDIS clients yet, you will, and fumbling with invoicing when your first NDIS participant walks in wastes time and looks unprofessional.

3. **Get clear on GST** with your accountant before you see your first NDIS participant. Not after.

4. **Create a standard service agreement template** and have it ready. Don't write one from scratch for each participant.

5. **Build relationships with 2-3 local support coordinators.** They're the gatekeepers for referrals in the NDIS space, and a good relationship with a support coordinator can provide a steady stream of participants.

6. **Understand the price guide** for your profession's support items. Know what you can charge, what you can claim for (including non-face-to-face time), and how to structure your invoices correctly.

The NDIS adds complexity to your practice, but it also adds a revenue stream that's largely independent of Medicare CDM. Participants who find a provider they trust tend to stay long-term. And unlike Medicare CDM, there's no 5-session annual cap. A well-run NDIS caseload can provide meaningful, stable income for your practice.
